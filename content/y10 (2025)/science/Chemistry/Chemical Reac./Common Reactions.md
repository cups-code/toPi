# Common Reactions
## Synthesis Reaction
- The combination of multiple reactants to form a single product.
- Synthesis reactions can be generalised using: $\ce{A + B -> AB}$
- An example of this would be $\ce{2H_2_{(g)} + O_2_{(g)} -> 2H_2O_{(l)}}$ .
## Decomposition Reaction
- The decomposition of a compound decomposing into two or more products.
- Decomposition reactions can be generalised using: $\ce{AB -> A + B}$
- This is basically the opposite of a synthesis reaction.
- An example of this would be $\ce{2H_2O_{(l)} -> 2H_2_{(g)} + O_2_{(g)}}$ .
	- This can be performed through electrolysis.
## Combustion Reactions
> [!Tip] Related Notes
> - This has already been covered in [[Reactions Involving Oxygen#Combustion Reactions|Combustion Reactions]].
- Combustion reactions involve the following and can be generalised into: $\ce{Hydrocarbon + Oxygen -> Carbon Dioxide + Water}$
	- Hydrocarbons like octane and methane are commonly used fuels in chemistry.
- This reaction is also **exothermic**, which means it always produces energy.
## Acid-Base Reactions
> [!Tip] Related Notes
> - This has already been covered in [[Acids and Bases#Neutralisation|Neutralisation Reactions]].
- To summarise, acid-base reactions are also called neutralisation reactions.
- This involves the following and can be generalised into: $\ce{Acid + Base -> Salt + Water}$
- An example of this would be: $\ce{NaOH + HCL -> NaCl + H_2O}$
	- $\ce{H2O}$ is collected through the taking of hydrogen ions ([[Bonding#Ionic Bonds|ionic bonding]] ?) 
	- $\ce{NaCl}$ is an ionic compound formed from an anion and cation.
## Acid-Carbonate Reactions
> [!Tip] Related Notes
> - This has already been covered in [[Acidic Reactions#Carbonate + Acid Reaction|Acid + Carbonate Reactions]].
- Acid and carbonate reactions involve the reaction between acids and carbonates. It involves the following and can be generalised into: $\ce{Acid + Carbonate -> Salt + Carbon Dioxide + Water}$ ^2dc22b
- An example of this $\ce{H2SO4_{(aq)} + CaCO3_{(s)} -> CaSO4_{(s)} + CO_2{(g)} + H2O{(l)}}$
	- In this example, we use sulfuric acid and calcium carbonate as our two reactants.
	- This forms a salt (cation from carbonate, anion from acid) called $\ce{CaSO4}$.
	- As determined by the general equation [[#^2dc22b|here]], the carbon dioxide and water is produced.
