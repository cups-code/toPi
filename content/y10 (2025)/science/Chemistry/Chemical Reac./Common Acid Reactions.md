# Common Acid Reactions
## Acid-Base / Alkali

> [!Tip] Related Notes
> - This has already been covered by [[Acids and Bases#Neutralisation]].
> - However, the current note covers alkalis and not only bases.

> [!Info] General Formula
> $$\ce{Acid + Alkali -> Salt + Water}$$

- An acid reacts with a base (or alkali, soluble bases) to form salt and water.
- The type of salt produced is dependent on the acid and base that react.
- The salt is an ionic solution (somewhat covered on [[Acid-Metal Reactions#^49c0b6|Acid-Metal Reactions]]), meaning that it is formed by a cation (from alkali) and anion (from acid).

> [!Tip] Utilising Neutralisation Reactions
> - In order to utilise the knowledge of acid-base/alkali (or neutralisation) reactions, you must learn these three specific acids (this was briefly covered [[Acid-Metal Reactions#Acids to Salts Chart|here]]).
> 	- Hydrochloric acid ($\ce{HCl}$) -> Chlorides
> 	- Nitric acid ($\ce{HNO3}$) -> Nitrates
> 	- Sulphuric acid ($\ce{H2SO4}$) -> Sulphates

## Acid-Carbonate

> [!Info] General Formula
> $$\ce{Acid + Metal Carbonate -> Salt + Water + Carbon Dioxide}$$
> - You can remember this formula by remembering the acronym "CSWAM" (copper + salt + water <- acid + metal)
> - This is also very similar to [[#Acid-Base / Alkali]] Reactions. The only difference is that since carbon is involved, carbon dioxide will be created.
> - The salt formed is made from the cation from metal carbonate and anion from the acid.

> [!Tip] Utilising Acid-Carbonate Reactions
> - In order to utilise the knowledge of acid-carbonate reactions, you must learn these three specific metal carbonates.
> 	- Sodium carbonate ($\ce{Na2CO3}$)
> 	- Potassium carbonate ($\ce{K2CO3}$)
> 	- Calcium carbonate ($\ce{CaCO3}$)
>
